# Food Earth — SQP Analysis (P0)

## Tagging Rationale

Tag application targeted ~10 queries that cover the dominant relevant search volume for the Six Flavor Variety Pack (B0D8Q9MMT9). Queries that primarily belong to other Food Earth products (e.g., "butter chicken sauce" for P1 Simmer Sauce, "chow mein noodles" for P3 Noodles, "soup" / "lentil soup" for P2 Soups) were excluded from tagging to keep the P0 analysis clean.

- **Tier 1 (Hero):** Queries where the customer is searching for ready-to-eat Indian food and P0 is a direct answer. Includes the broad "indian food" query (P0 is one of the top intent answers) and two competitor-brand searches ("tasty bites indian food", "deep indian kitchen") where shoppers are clearly hunting for the exact product format P0 sells. 3 queries tagged.
- **Tier 2 (Core market):** Queries for ready-to-eat / microwave meals broadly, where P0 is one solution among many (alongside frozen meals, MREs, Kevin's Natural Foods bowls, etc.). The cuisine intent is unspecified. 5 queries tagged.
- **Tier 3 (Broad/adjacent):** "indian" (very broad, could be any Indian product) and "biryani" (specific to one of the 6 flavors in the variety pack, plus there is a standalone Vegetable Biryani child). Lower intent / lower fit. 2 queries tagged.

### Phase 5: Catalog Overlap Check

- **Tier 1:** "indian food" can be served by multiple Food Earth products (Ready-to-Eat Bundle, Indian Simmer Sauce, Indian Dips). However, the two competitor-brand queries ("tasty bites indian food", "deep indian kitchen") strongly indicate the ready-to-eat tray format = P0's territory. Mixed overlap, treat the **adjusted cap as ~16%** (assuming 2 products on average could rank).
- **Tier 2:** "ready to eat meals" can be served by P0, the Soup parent (P2), and the Noodles parent (P3). 3 products could rank. **Adjusted cap ~24%.**
- **Tier 3:** "biryani" is specific to P0's Vegetable Biryani child (and the variety pack contains it). "indian" is very broad. **Cap ~8-9% for biryani specifically, variable for "indian".**

### Why CVR is the killer (Tier 1)

CVR is 5.13% (brand) vs 20.7% (industry) on Tier 1 queries. The 4x gap is the binding constraint on growth. Three things drive this gap:

1. **Rating (per Step 2):** P0 sits at 3.7 stars. Competitors (Tasty Bite, Maya Kaimal, Kitchens of India) are at 4.2-4.5. A 0.5-0.8 star gap on a food product where taste matters is a major conversion killer.
2. **Listing presentation gaps (per Step 2):** Image #2 is a wasted brand-authorization certificate, no video, weak A+ comparison module. These are fixable, but their combined CVR impact is incremental, not transformative.
3. **Product-query fit:** "indian food" is searched by people looking for groceries, takeout substitutes, snacks, sauces, and ready meals. P0 only converts the ready-meals slice of that intent.

The brand CTR (1.35% vs 1.89%) is only modestly below industry, meaning the main image / title / price combination is roughly competitive at the search-results level. Shoppers click in roughly normal proportions. They drop off later in the funnel, which points to PDP-level issues (rating, A+, video) rather than thumbnail-level issues.

### Impression Share is also a constraint, but it is downstream of CVR

Tier 1 impression share grew from 0.10% to 0.62% over 3 months as ad spend ramped. There is room to grow another ~25x to reach the adjusted cap (~16%). But each additional impression converts at 5% (brand) vs 20% (industry), so the unit economics get worse with each scaling step. Until CVR closes, scaling impressions is paying a CVR penalty. Per CLAUDE.md's "Low impression share + poor CVR" pattern, the order is: fix CVR drivers first, then deploy PPC capital to scale visibility.

## Insights

- **P0 (Six Flavor Variety Pack) is winning the visibility battle on biryani-specific queries (~1% impression share on a 1.5k-volume query) but losing on its biggest tier.** Tier 1 ("indian food", competitor-brand searches) is where the volume is (26k/mo) and the brand has only 0.3% share. The visibility growth from the ad ramp is real but the conversion engine behind it isn't ready.
- **The biggest seasonal opportunity is Q4 2026 and it is 4-5 months away.** Tier 1 search volume rises ~50% from Apr/May lows to Nov/Dec/Jan peaks. The brand needs CVR fixes, buy box stability, and ad budget planned by September. Last year's Q4 was lost to the buy box collapse documented in Step 1.
- **Tier 2 is not a winnable tier with current product.** The brand has 0.05% impression share against a $890k/mo market, but CVR is 1.45% vs industry 20.8%. Even with massive PPC investment, the variety pack does not satisfy what "ready to eat meals" searchers want (they are largely searching for non-ethnic options like Kevin's Natural Food, frozen meals, or MREs). Time spent here is wasted relative to Tier 1.
- **Tier 3 biryani is a small but cheap incremental win.** Brand impression share on "biryani" is around 25-65% (already high), CTR is above industry, but CVR lags. Could yield ~50-100 additional purchases per year at low spend if the standalone Vegetable Biryani child (B086WPJGXQ) is positioned for it.

## Things to Investigate Further (for Step 4)

- **Are we already bidding on "indian food" and the competitor-brand terms ("tasty bites", "deep indian kitchen")?** If not, those are the first PPC keywords to add. If we are bidding, what is the CPC and where is the budget actually flowing? Per Step 1, ~98% of P0's ad spend is going to the Vegetable Biryani child, not the Variety Pack hero. Confirm in ad data.
- **What is the wasted spend on irrelevant Tier 2 queries?** If campaigns are bidding on broad "ready to eat meals" terms, those clicks convert at 1.45%. That spend should be pulled and redirected to Tier 1.
- **What is the placement breakdown of current P0 ad spend?** Brand CTR is just below industry on Tier 1, but the share data suggests new impressions are coming with worse CTR. That points to non-Top-of-Search placements where conversion is structurally lower.
