## Why Merging Is a Growth Lever

1. **Review pooling.** Each listing currently has its own rating, ranging from 1.8 to 5.0. The low-rated child B0F4R69LGK (1.8) is poisoning its listing and likely dragging share. When variants are consolidated under one parent, review counts aggregate and the average smooths out. The combined ASIN would show 4.x across ~hundreds of reviews rather than fragmented 3-4x across small pools.
2. **Impression share consolidation.** Today on "fly fishing glasses" and related queries, a search impression shows one of Hippo's 3 parents at a time; the other two are invisible. Merged, any of the variants can surface under one listing, effectively multiplying the share cap. Step 3 below shows this explicitly: Tier 1 adjusted cap becomes ~16-18% merged, up from the ~8-9% single-product ceiling.
3. **Better conversion via variation selector.** Shoppers who click a merged listing self-select magnifying vs sport vs clip vs magnet release. That is a native CVR lift on an already-strong funnel (Tier 1 brand CVR is 10.1% vs industry 6.84%).
4. **Ad structure simplifies.** Step 4 identified the "Fishing Glasses 2025" catch-all campaign mixing P1 and P2 children. A merged parent ASIN allows one cleanly structured campaign per lens-type variant, with each getting a dedicated budget and bid control.
5. **SEO authority consolidation.** Amazon's organic ranking for a parent aggregates the child-level sales velocity. Three separate parents compete with each other for rank; one merged parent gets credit for the combined velocity.
6. **Broken parent shell (B0F5QHC9BW) gets swept up in the fix.** The P2 parent shell is a non-selling, 0% buy-box, no-A+, no-video phantom listing (same hygiene issue as the P0 parent shell B0CM72RSS8). A proper merge includes suppressing this shell.

## Product Profile (Combined P1+P2)

**Product:** A family of fly-fishing-specific glasses with a shared attachment system (clip-on nose, magnetic release, or lanyard) and interchangeable lens types. Two primary use cases:
1. **Magnifying:** slim reading-glasses-style magnifiers worn on the nose via clip or magnetic release so the angler can quickly flip them into place to tie on a fly or inspect a tippet, without fumbling to put on full-size reading glasses. One-hand-use is the key design principle (the other hand holds the rod).
2. **Sport polarized:** same attachment design but polarized lenses for reading water and spotting fish.

**Customer:** Fly anglers, older skew (magnification-needing), destination-fishing oriented (trout, salmon streams). The "vest angler" who needs small, fast, lightweight accessories that clip to a vest or lanyard. Many will buy both a magnifying pair and a sport pair from the same brand once they own one.

**Brand:** Hippo Outdoor (Benares AB, Swedish). Same brand as the tarp line but a genuinely distinct product category. The fly-fishing product line is the most brand-forward part of the catalog: better titles ("HIPPO OUTDOOR" prefix across P2 children), video on the P1 listing, and clearer feature communication than the tarp line. Hippo clearly has more invested presentation-wise in the fly-fishing line than in tarps.

**Competitive Landscape:**
- Fly-fishing-specific magnifiers are a small, specialized category. Primary competitors are FlipFocals, Fastflies ReadyReaders, Orvis Fly Fishing Magnifiers, and generic Amazon-brand magnifiers marketed to anglers.
- Fly-fishing sport glasses compete with Smith Optics, Costa Del Mar, and a long tail of Amazon-brand polarized fishing glasses. This is a more crowded, mostly premium-priced segment ($80-200 for Costa/Smith vs Hippo's ~$12-13).
- **Price positioning:** Hippo sits at ~$12-13 across both variants. This is aggressively underpriced in the polarized-sport-glasses category (where premium brands start at $80) and mid-market in the fly-fishing magnifier category ($10-20). Price is not the blocker; if anything Hippo may be underpricing the sport variants.
