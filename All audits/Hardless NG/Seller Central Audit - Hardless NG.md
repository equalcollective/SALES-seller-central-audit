# Seller Central Audit - Hardless NG

> **Scope note (read first).** Hardless NG's Seller Central business reports are not connected to our system, so this audit does not yet include sales, sessions, organic-vs-ad split, buy box, or true conversion rate. It is built from three sources we do have: 89 days of advertising data (Feb to May 2026), 18 months of Search Query Performance data, and the full Keepa product history. Even without the business reports, the picture is clear and actionable. Connecting Seller Central is the one step that would let us put exact dollar figures on everything below, and it is the natural reason to get on a call.

---

## The Headline

Hardless NG sells a compact, salt-free, whole-house water conditioner, a budget alternative to bulky salt-tank softeners. The listing is well-built and the product has consistent demand. But the account has a clear, fixable growth problem hiding in plain sight:

1. **You win the click and lose the sale.** On your core "water softener" searches, shoppers click your product **more** than the category average, then buy it far **less** (conversion is roughly one-seventh of the market). People are interested in salt-free, then hesitate.
2. **You are nearly invisible in the market you most belong in.** "Whole house water filter" is an ~85,000-searches-per-month, roughly $774K/month market that your product is a literal answer to, and you hold **0.05%** of the visibility.
3. **Your ad budget is funding the wrong things.** About 75% of spend runs below break-even, your best-converting ad placement gets only 7% of budget, and you are paying for generic searches that do not convert while your proven terms run 4x to 9x return on tiny budgets.

None of these require a new product. They require fixing conversion (perception and rating), claiming visibility where you fit, and redirecting spend you are already making.

## Section 2: Qualitative Product Understanding (P0)

**Product:**
- A compact, salt-free whole-house water filter and conditioner that reduces limescale, sediment, chlorine, and heavy metals while keeping beneficial minerals. A single inline canister that connects to the main water line.
- Value prop: does the job of a bulky salt softener at a fraction of the size, cost, and maintenance (no salt, no plumber, no tank).
- Purchase motivation: hard-water pain (limescale, soap scum, dry skin/hair) plus an unwillingness to install or maintain a traditional softener.

**Customer:**
- Practical, budget-conscious homeowners with hard water, plus a meaningful RV / camper segment (clear in the search data).
- Driver: "I have hard water but I do not want a giant salt system."

**Brand:**
- Registered brand with a real identity: own website, support phone line on the product, Amazon Brand Store, off-Amazon retail and financing partners.
- Established and Amazon-native (product line goes back to the NG3). Utilitarian, problem-solver vibe; clean engineering aesthetic.

**Competitive Landscape:**
- Price positioning: Hardless is the budget / compact end, roughly $130 to $200. Premium salt-free systems (Filtersmart, Kind Water, SpringWell) run $600 to $1,200+. Traditional salt softeners (Whirlpool, GE, Rheem) run $400 to $900 and actually soften via ion exchange.
- Key differentiator: size and price. Key gap: it is a conditioner, not a true softener, so it does not change a hardness reading. That is the central perception challenge with "water softener" shoppers.

**Listing Quality:**

*Strengths:*
- Title: 199 characters, brand included, no stuffing, leads with the salt-free angle.
- Bullets: 5 full bullets, each with an all-caps benefit hook covering the core selling points.
- A+ Content: present and Premium (7 modules, 11 images).
- Video: 2 videos. Brand Store: present. Clean hero image with inlet-size badge.

*Opportunities:*
- Rating (4.0): the genuine weak spot, down from ~4.5 in 2024. For a product going on the main water line, 4.0 dampens trust and conversion.
- Main image / A+ messaging: does not visually answer the "is a salt-free unit a real softener?" question. Adding a cutaway and a "Salt-Free, Retains Essential Minerals" proof point would address the exact hesitation the data shows.

**The key nuance:** the listing is not thin or lazy. The conversion gap is driven by **product perception and rating**, not missing content. The fix is trust and reviews, not rewriting bullets.

> **Solution:** Archive the dead campaigns, rebuild around a clean NG4 vs NG4L structure with the converting terms broken into dedicated campaigns.

## Section 6: Action Plan

The primary blocker is **conversion on Tier 1** (you win clicks, lose sales), with a clean **visibility whitespace on Tier 2** (whole house water filter). Because this is pay-per-click, the sequence matters: stop the bleed and concentrate spend on what already converts first (fast PPC wins), then fix the conversion drivers (rating and perception) so the brand can scale into Tiers 1 and 2 profitably.

#### Weeks 1-2: Immediate Actions (PPC, stop the bleed)
- Pause / rebuild the unprofitable NG4 keyword and complements campaigns (~$5,894 at ~1.0x ROAS).
- Negate the mismatched search terms (hard water filter, well water filter, water filter system, plain water softener) and the salt/cleaner terms.
- Raise Top-of-Search bid modifiers to shift budget out of Rest of Search (5.34 ROAS vs 1.04).
- Archive the ~56 dormant sub-$10 campaigns to clean up the account.

#### Weeks 2-4: Short-Term Optimizations (PPC, concentrate on winners)
- Break the proven converters (whole house water softener, salt free water softener system, whole house water filter) into dedicated, well-funded exact campaigns.
- Rebalance budget from NG4 toward the converting NG4L structure.
- Stand up a small dedicated campaign for the Replacement Cartridges (P1/P2), which already run 27x to 62x ROAS, to grow the recurring-revenue annuity.
- Begin listing-content prep (writing, not publishing): main-image cutaway + "salt-free, retains minerals" proof, A+ trust module addressing the "is this a real softener?" question.

#### Weeks 4-6: Medium-Term Growth (listing + conversion)
- Publish the listing improvements (main image, A+ trust module, expectation-setting copy).
- Launch a review-generation push on P0 to lift the 4.0 rating (the single biggest conversion lever).
- Begin building Tier 2 impression share on "whole house water filter" terms now that conversion support is in place.
- Monitor CVR impact from the listing and rating changes.

#### Weeks 6-8: Scaling and Evaluation
- Scale PPC on the improved listing across the matched Tier 1 and Tier 2 terms.
- Reassess Tier 1 impression share (target: recover from ~1% back toward the prior ~3%).
- Evaluate whether the under-sink filter (P3) warrants its own launch.

## Section 7: Insights & Questions for the Seller

**Insights:**
- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) wins the click but loses the sale on Tier 1.** Brand CTR beats the market (1.69% vs 1.31%) while CVR trails badly (0.26% vs 1.93%). The bottleneck is conversion and trust (4.0 rating + the "is salt-free a real softener?" gap), not the listing content or search appeal. This is the most important finding in the audit.
- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) is nearly invisible in the market it most directly serves (Tier 2, whole house water filter).** 0.05% impression share on a ~$774K/month market the product is a literal answer to, and it out-converts industry on the matched query. Clean, fast upside.
- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) ad spend is misallocated, not mis-structured.** ~75% of spend runs below break-even, the NG4 variant bleeds while NG4L converts, Rest of Search eats 61% of budget at 1.04 ROAS while Top of Search prints 5.34, and generic terms draw clicks but not sales. The converting terms (4x to 9x ROAS) are already visible in the data.
- **P1/P2 (Replacement Cartridges) are an untapped recurring-revenue lever.** 27x to 62x ROAS on under $80 of spend. Almost no budget behind a product customers must rebuy every 6 to 8 months.

**Questions for the Seller:**
- On "water softener" searches your product gets clicked more than average but converts far less. Our read: shoppers expect a salt-based softener and hesitate when they see it is salt-free, compounded by the 4.0 rating. Does that match your customer questions and reviews? The answer decides whether we lead with listing expectation-setting, a review push, or leaning into "whole house filter" demand.
- The NG4 (regular hard water) campaigns convert far worse than NG4L (extra hard water). Is there a known difference in price, reviews, or demand between the variants, or should we simply shift budget to NG4L?
- The hero rating sits at ~4.0, down from ~4.5 in 2024. Was there a product, supplier, or packaging change, or is this the natural review mix for a salt-free conditioner? It determines whether the fix is product, listing, or reviews.
- Can you connect Seller Central (or share a Business Report export) before the call? It is the one missing piece, and it would let us size the exact revenue impact of everything above.
