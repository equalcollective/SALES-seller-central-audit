# SQP Analysis - P0: Hardless NG Whole House Water Filter

## Tagging Rationale

SQP is brand-level, so the brand's query universe was isolated into three tiers relevant to the P0 system. 18 queries tagged. Highly relevant but clearly different-product queries (water softener **salt**, water softener **cleaner**, salt **for** water softener, water softener **shower head**, laundry softener) were deliberately left untagged: a salt-free conditioner cannot serve a salt buyer, and these terms are a source of wasted impressions, not opportunity.

- **Tier 1 (Hero, salt-free softener identity):** `water softener`, `water softener system`, `water softeners`, `whole house water softener`, `whole home water softener`, `salt free water softener`, `salt free water softener system`. The brand's own positioning. This is the market it actively competes in today and where it has built the most footprint.
- **Tier 2 (Core market, whole-house filtration):** `whole house water filter`, `whole house water filter system`, `whole house water filtration system`, `whole house filter`, `water filters whole house`. The product literally IS a whole-house water filter, but it barely shows up here. This is the cleanest whitespace.
- **Tier 3 (Broad / adjacent):** `water filter`, `water filter system`, `water purifier`, `well water filter`, `hard water filter`, `shower filter`. Huge volume but low intent match. Most of these searchers want pitcher, faucet, or shower filters, not a whole-house system.

**Catalog overlap check (Phase 5):** Only the P0 system ranks for these tier queries. The under-sink filter and cartridges do not compete on "water softener" or "whole house water filter" terms. So **1 product ranks per tier, impression-share cap stays ~8-9%.** No upward adjustment. This makes the low impression shares below genuinely low, not a data artifact.

**Key observations:**

- **Tier 1 impression share has roughly halved over 9 months.** Monthly progression: ~2.0% (May 25), peaking ~3.0% (Sep 25), then falling to ~0.9 to 1.0% (Mar to Apr 26). The brand is losing visibility on its own core market, and against a ~8-9% cap it is now capturing barely an eighth of what one product can.
- **Tier 2 is wide-open whitespace.** A ~$774K/month market that the product is a literal match for, and Hardless holds 0.05% impression share. It simply is not showing up.
- **Share falls at every funnel stage.** On Tier 1, impression share (1.08%) is already low, and it erodes further to click share (0.98%), cart share (0.56%), and purchase share (0.11%). The brand loses ground at each step, which points to a conversion problem stacked on top of a visibility problem.

## Blockers and Growth Path

Rates are volume-weighted. Where the 3-month brand base is too thin for purchase-rate confidence, the 12-month window and the add-to-cart stage (which reaches significance faster) are used, per methodology.

| Tier | Impression Share | CTR (Brand vs Industry) | CVR (Brand vs Industry) | Primary Blocker | Growth Path |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Tier 1 | 1.08% of ~8-9% (declining) | 1.69% vs 1.31% (above) | 0.26% vs 1.93% (12-mo) | **CVR**, with declining impression share secondary | Low impression share + poor CVR. Fix conversion first (perception + rating), then rebuild visibility via PPC on the converting salt-free terms |
| Tier 2 | 0.05% of ~8-9% | 1.25% vs 1.41% (near industry) | too thin (brand barely present) | **Impression Share** | PPC scaling + listing SEO. The product matches the query; it just is not bidding/ranking here |
| Tier 3 | 0.01% of ~8-9% | 1.64% vs 1.33% (above) | ~0% vs 11-12% | Intent mismatch | Mostly not capturable. Different product intent (pitcher/faucet/shower). Skip as a focus |

**Reading the blockers:**

- **Tier 1 is the "win the click, lose the sale" problem.** Brand CTR is consistently **above** industry (1.69% vs 1.31% over 12 months). Shoppers are genuinely intrigued by the salt-free angle and click. But the brand then converts at roughly **one-seventh** of industry (CVR 0.26% vs 1.93%), and the gap is visible at the cart stage too (ATC 6.2% vs 11.5%, on a solid base of 507 brand cart-adds). This is not a visibility-only story. People arrive, get interested, and do not buy. The most likely drivers (from Step 2): the 4.0 rating and the "is a salt-free unit a real softener?" trust gap. Because this is a pay-per-click model, scaling impressions before fixing conversion would just burn budget.
- **Tier 2 is the cleanest growth lever.** Here the product is a direct match (it is a whole-house water filter), the market is ~$774K/month, and the blocker is purely that Hardless does not show up (0.05% impression share). Where the brand does appear on the closely related "whole house water softener" query, it converts **above** industry (1.12% vs 0.85% CVR). That is the tell: when intent matches the product, it works. The path is straightforward, claim impression share via PPC and listing SEO on "whole house water filter" terms.
- **Tier 3 is a trap.** Enormous volume ($10M+/month) but the searchers want pitcher, faucet, or shower filters. Brand CVR is effectively zero on these. Do not chase it.

## Insights

- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) wins the click but loses the cart on Tier 1.** Brand CTR beats industry (1.69% vs 1.31%) while CVR trails badly (0.26% vs 1.93%). The bottleneck is conversion and trust, not search-results-page appeal. This is the single most important finding in the audit.
- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) is nearly invisible on the market it most directly serves (Tier 2, whole house water filter).** 0.05% impression share on a ~$774K/month market that the product is a literal answer to. The one place the brand reliably out-converts industry is the matched "whole house water softener" query, confirming the opportunity is real.
- **P0 (Whole House Water Filter) Tier 1 impression share has halved since summer 2025** (~3.0% down to ~1.0%). Visibility on the core market is eroding.

## Questions for the Seller

- On "water softener" searches, your product gets clicked more than the category average but converts far less. Our read is shoppers expect a salt-based softener and hesitate when they realize it is a salt-free conditioner, compounded by the 4.0 rating. Does that match what you see in customer questions and reviews? The answer decides whether we lead with expectation-setting in the listing, a review push, or repositioning onto "whole house filter" demand.
