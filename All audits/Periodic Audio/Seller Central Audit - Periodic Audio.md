# Seller Central Audit - Periodic Audio

**Prepared for the second sales call.** Periodic Audio is a credible, engineering-led audiophile brand (founded 2016, ex-Apple/Dolby/Harman team, 45+ patents) selling on Amazon through just 3 accessory SKUs. The account is small: trailing 12-month sales are about $4,200 (~$346/mo), effectively all from one product, the $49 Rhodium USB-C DAC. The brand has never run Amazon ads. The opportunity and the honest constraints are laid out below.

## Section 2: Qualitative Product Understanding (P0 - Rhodium USB-C DAC/Amp)

**Product:**
- An ultra-compact (6 gram) USB-C dongle DAC/amplifier that adds a high-resolution headphone jack to any phone, tablet, or computer. USB-A adapter included.
- Specs: up to 32-bit/384kHz, 113 dB dynamic range, THD under 0.007%, 31 mW at 32 ohms. Audiophile-grade components (C0G/X7R caps, custom high-flex cable).
- Value prop: noticeably better sound than a device's built-in DAC, in the smallest form factor on the market.
- Purchase motivation: headphone enthusiasts who want better mobile sound, plus mainstream users who lost their headphone jack and want a quality replacement.

**Customer:**
- Audiophiles and headphone hobbyists (the Head-Fi crowd), plus jack-less-phone owners who care about audio.
- Driver: "better sound from my wired headphones, on the go, without a bulky dongle."

**Brand:**
- Registered, credible, DTC-first brand with real equity in the enthusiast community. Founded 2016, Ventura CA. Founders from Apple, Dolby, Harman, Sonos, Blue Microphones; 45+ patents.
- Full line (element-named IEMs, multiple DACs) lives on periodicaudio.com. **Only 3 accessory SKUs are on Amazon**, a small slice of the brand.
- Vibe: engineering-led, "the science of sound in your pocket," periodic-table motif. Credible, not flashy.

**Competitive Landscape:**
- Price positioning: real USB-C DAC dongles average ~$55-60 (FiiO KA11 ~$33, Rhodium $49, iFi GO link $59, AudioQuest DragonFly $99-170). The Rhodium at $49 is mid-tier, slightly below average. But against shoppers who just want a cheap USB-C-to-3.5mm adapter (Apple ~$9), it is 4-5x the price.

| Competitor | Product | Approx Price | Note |
|-----------|---------|------|------|
| Apple | USB-C to 3.5mm | ~$9 | Commodity volume leader, not a real DAC |
| FiiO | KA11 | ~$33 | More power, big review base |
| iFi | GO link / Max | $59-79 | Balanced output, audiophile cred |
| AudioQuest | DragonFly | $99-170 | Premium tier, strong recall |

- Differentiators: smallest/lightest (6g), genuine pedigree, USA components.
- Gaps: lower output power (31 mW vs 200 mW+ on newer rivals), no balanced output, no volume controls/screen, far fewer reviews (101 vs thousands). Being out-spec'd and out-reviewed.

**Listing Quality:**

*Strengths:*
- Premium A+ content (6 modules, 7 images) and a live Brand Store.
- Title is clean: 116 chars, brand + use cases, no stuffing.

*Opportunities:*
- **Main image (top priority):** shows the product as a plain braided adapter cable + USB-A adapter on white. It looks identical to a $9 commodity dongle, with no "Hi-Res DAC" cue and no headphones. It neither earns the click nor justifies $49. Add a "High-Res Audio DAC" overlay and show it connected to headphones.
- **Image count:** only 3 (Amazon allows 7-9). Add size-in-hand, in-use, what's-in-box, specs graphic, and a "beats your phone's built-in DAC" comparison.
- **Video:** the only video is a 10-second customer clip titled "STUCK IN USB C PORT." No brand video. Add a 30-second brand video (sound-quality story + correct fit/removal).
- **Bullets:** only 4 of 5 used, not benefit-led, one is 271 dense characters. Rewrite to 5 scannable, benefit-led bullets.

**Key SQP observations:**

## Section 6: Action Plan (Ad-Launch Strategy)

The blocker is impression share: the brand is invisible in non-branded search. The fast lever is PPC. But this is a pay-per-click model and the product's conversion on cold traffic is unproven and at real risk (4.0 rating, 101 reviews, a commodity-looking image). So the sequence is deliberately **listing first, then paid entry on the narrowest high-intent terms, then scale only if it converts.** Budgets are intentionally small to match the account.

### Weeks 1-2: Fix the listing (do this before spending on ads)
- **Rebuild the main image:** add a "High-Res Audio DAC" text overlay and show the dongle connected to headphones, so it reads as a premium DAC, not a $9 adapter. This drives both CTR and the credibility to hold $49. (Ref: Section 2 listing opportunity.)
- **Expand to 7+ images:** size-in-hand, in-use with phone + headphones, what's-in-box, specs/benefits graphic, "beats your phone's built-in DAC" comparison.
- **Add a 30-second brand video** covering the sound-quality story and correct fit/removal (also blunts the "stuck in port" customer clip).
- **Rewrite bullets** into 5 benefit-led, scannable lines.

### Weeks 2-4: Launch paid, tightly targeted
- **Sponsored Products, exact-match manual, Tier 1 only:** usb c dac, portable dac, dongle dac, dac dongle, usb dac. 3-5 keywords per campaign, controlled bids. Start ~$10-15/day total. These are the terms where a $49 audiophile DAC is a believable answer.
- **One auto campaign** (small budget) for discovery, to harvest converting search terms.
- **Small branded-defense campaign** on "periodic audio" / "rhodium" (2-3% of budget): competitors take ~90% of impressions on the brand's own name. Protective only, do not scale it (branded is not growth).
- **Set a clear kill criterion:** if Tier 1 terms do not convert at an acceptable ACoS within the test budget, stop and treat the issue as conversion (reviews/price/product), not visibility.

### Weeks 4-6: Harvest and extend (only if Tier 1 converts)
- Move converting auto search terms into exact-match campaigns.
- Add the strongest Tier 2 terms (dac amp, headphone amp for pc) at low bids.
- Layer in Sponsored Display product targeting: defensively on the brand's own listing, and offensively against close competitor ASINs (e.g. FiiO KA11) where the Rhodium's portability is a real differentiator.

### Weeks 6-8: Scale or conclude
- Scale spend on whatever clears the ACoS target on the improved listing.
- If non-branded CVR stays poor despite the listing fixes, the honest conclusion is that the Amazon channel ceiling for this single product is low. The real levers then become review generation (4.0 to 4.4+) and possibly a product refresh (more power / balanced output), which is a brand/product decision, not a PPC one.
- Evaluate bringing more of the catalog (IEMs, other DACs) onto Amazon, which would matter far more than squeezing this one SKU.

**Budget reality:** a $300-450/mo ad test will initially exceed the product's current revenue. Frame it honestly as an investment to find out whether the large non-branded DAC market is capturable, with a defined kill criterion, not as a guaranteed return on a $350/mo product.

## Section 7: Insights & Questions for the Seller

**Insights:**
- P0 (Rhodium DAC) is ~99% of the Amazon business; P1 (Neon Receiver) is dormant and P2 is dead. The account is effectively one product.
- P0 (Rhodium DAC) holds ~0% of a ~$184k/mo non-branded DAC market and lives almost entirely on branded and off-Amazon demand (~75% of sales come from outside captured search). Entering non-branded search is the entire growth thesis.
- The blocker is impression share, but conversion on cold traffic is unproven and at risk: a 4.0 rating vs 4.4-4.7 competitors, only 101 reviews, and a main image that looks like a $9 commodity adapter. Listing fixes must come before scaling spend, or PPC will burn money.
- Sessions have roughly halved year-over-year. With no acquisition engine, organic visibility will keep eroding.
- The biggest lever may sit outside this listing entirely: only 3 of the brand's products are on Amazon. The full catalog (IEMs, other DACs) is absent.

**Questions for the Seller:**
- The core catalog (element-named IEMs, Nickel and BlueDAC) is not on Amazon, only 3 accessory SKUs. Are those sold elsewhere, discontinued, or is there an opportunity to bring the full line to Amazon? This likely matters more than optimizing the Rhodium alone.
- About three-quarters of Amazon sales come from outside captured search. Is Amazon a deliberate secondary channel behind your DTC site, or a channel you want to actively grow? This decides whether a paid-acquisition investment is worth it.
- The rating slid from ~4.6 to 4.0 and the only product video is a "stuck in USB-C port" complaint. Is the plug retention/durability a known issue?
- $49 has held for 18+ months while a more powerful FiiO sells at ~$33. Is there margin flexibility on price?
- Confirming: you have never run Amazon ads (we see zero history). Is that correct, and is paid acquisition something you are open to?
